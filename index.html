The Self Reference Effect

This study explored if the self-reference effect links to the retrieval practice concept. Previous work suggested that deeply processing information strengthens memory. In an experiment by Rogers et al. (1977), words describing the self were better remembered than those judged superficially. Although the self-reference effect aids memory, its exact workings remain unclear. This study proposed that in the self-reference condition, people might repeat words to themselves, aiding later recall. Two hypotheses were tested: one suggesting improvement in recalling traits through repetition, and another proposing repetition's impact across conditions. Experiments 1a, 1b, and 1c were conducted online with participants from Amazon Mechanical Turk Masterâ€™s and Brooklyn College SONA. Results supported the first hypothesis, revealing improved recall with repetition, but the self-reference effect only emerged without retrieval practice. However, since direct measurement of participants' word repetition was lacking, other factors might contribute to the self-reference effect. Future research could delve deeper into the role of retrieval processes in this paradigm
